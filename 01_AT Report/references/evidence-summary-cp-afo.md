# Evidence summary — AFOs for Cerebral Palsy

Citation bank for custom-AFO reports in CP (no MS/FES content here — kept separate deliberately since they don't share a clinical argument).

| Source | Focus | Use it to support |
|---|---|---|
| Ankle-foot orthoses among children with cerebral palsy — cross-sectional study, BMC Musculoskeletal Disorders (2023) | Multi-country registry: how commonly AFOs are prescribed by GMFCS level, CP subtype, age | Establishing AFO provision as standard of care in CP, not an unusual request |
| Efficacy of Lower Limb Orthoses in the Rehabilitation of Children with Cerebral Palsy — systematic review, *Children* (MDPI, 2023) | AFO/KAFO effects on walking, balance, spasticity, contracture prevention, quality of life | Direct clinical-effectiveness citation: "AFOs improve gait, balance, and can reduce energy cost in children with CP" |
| The influence of ankle-foot orthoses on gait pathology in children with cerebral palsy — *Gait & Posture* (2022) | AFO effects across heterogeneous CP gait patterns | Justifying a *custom*, tailored AFO over an off-the-shelf one — CP gait patterns are not uniform, so device type must match the individual's specific deviation |
| Principles of Ankle-Foot Orthosis Prescription in Ambulatory Bilateral Cerebral Palsy — review, *Physical Medicine & Rehabilitation Clinics* | Clinical guidance on choosing AFO type (solid, posterior leaf spring, dynamic, ground-reaction, hinged) by deformity/tone/functional goal | Clinical rationale for the specific device type selected |
| Design principles, manufacturing and evaluation techniques of custom dynamic AFOs — review (2022); evaluation of AFOs using experimental/computational methods (2023) | Materials, stiffness, wall thickness, carbon-fibre vs thermoplastic, mechanical testing | Justifying custom fabrication, material choice, and expected longevity/durability |
| Population/registry studies of AFO use in CP (e.g. Sweden registry, BMC 2014) | Prevalence and outcomes of AFO use by age/GMFCS | Backup evidence that AFO provision is well-established long-term practice, not experimental |

## Why custom, not off-the-shelf (the recurring justification point)

Where a participant has foot deformity or prominent bony anatomy, off-the-shelf AFOs are generally unsuitable — they won't accommodate the individual shape, causing poor fit, discomfort, and reduced effectiveness. State this specifically (which deformity, which prominence) rather than generically; it's the detail that distinguishes "clinically necessary" from "preference."
