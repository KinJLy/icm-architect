# Evidence summary — FES for MS-related foot drop

Citation bank for Section 5 (Supporting Evidence) of an MS + Bioness/FES report. Cite with inline author/year every time — see `submission-compliance-checklist.md`.

## Quantitative findings

| Study | Finding | NDIS relevance |
|---|---|---|
| Miller et al. (2017) | 0.05–0.08 m/s increase in gait speed on short-walk tests (initial and ongoing orthotic effect) | FES is a clinically effective, durable alternative to passive AFOs |
| Street & Singleton (2018) — 5-year cohort | 42% of users improved ≥1 functional walking category at 6 months; 29% retained that improvement at 5 years. Unassisted walking speed declined over 5 years (~0.58→0.46 m/s) but the *orthotic effect* was maintained (~0.52 m/s with FES vs 0.46 m/s without) | Demonstrates long-term durability of benefit, relevant to a progressive condition |
| Barr et al. (2017) | Improved ankle/hip power and neuromuscular control under fatigued conditions after 8 weeks of use | Justifies FES specifically where gait deteriorates with neurological fatigue |
| Taylor et al. (2010) | 12–24% reduction in physiological cost of walking | Supports value-for-money — walking requires measurably less effort |
| Renfrew et al. (2019), RCT | Improvements in walking performance, functional mobility, participant-reported mobility | Meaningful functional benefit for appropriately selected participants |
| Renfrew et al. (2019, 2020), qualitative/QoL review | Increased confidence, reduced fear of falling, improved participation and independence — often reported even where objective gait-speed change was modest | Participation-related outcomes matter as much as biomechanical ones |
| Meta-analysis, 19 studies (~490 participants with MS) | ~0.05 m/s initial gait-speed increase, ~0.08 m/s with ongoing use (short-walk tests) | Broad evidence base, not a single-study claim |

## Trials / evidence packs

- **NCT02123537** — completed clinical trial of the Bioness L300 system specifically in MS with foot drop (device-specific evidence, not just FES-in-general).
- **MS Society UK FES Evidence Pack (2022)** — summary evidence pack for FES in MS-related foot drop, useful as a credible third-party source for assessors.
- **NICE guidance — Functional Electrical Stimulation for Drop Foot** — regulatory/clinical-guideline backing.

## Ready-to-use paragraph

> "Functional Electrical Stimulation (FES), including devices such as the Bioness L300, is an evidence-based intervention for the management of foot drop in individuals with Multiple Sclerosis. FES facilitates dorsiflexion during swing phase by stimulating the peroneal nerve, improving toe clearance and promoting a more physiological gait pattern. Clinical studies and systematic reviews demonstrate that FES use in MS is associated with improvements in gait speed, walking endurance, and functional mobility, including clinically meaningful changes in timed walking tests and 6-minute walk performance. Longer-term use has also been associated with improvements in neuromuscular control and reduced fatigue-related gait deterioration, supporting its role not only as an orthotic aid but also as a gait re-education tool."

## AFO vs FES comparison (for the alternatives-considered section)

| Feature | AFO | FES (Bioness) |
|---|---|---|
| Gait pattern | Mechanical restriction | Physiological activation |
| Muscle activation | None (passive) | Active neuromuscular recruitment |
| Fatigue adaptation | Fixed, doesn't adjust | Dynamic — maintains dorsiflexion under fatigue |
| Terrain adaptability | Limited | Higher |
| Neuro-retraining potential | No | Yes (activity-dependent, variable across users) |
| Energy efficiency | Variable | Often improved |

## Device-tier notes (if the case involves an L300 → L300 Go / Go Plus upgrade)

L300 Go adds integrated 3D motion sensing (no external foot switch), improved real-time gait-phase adaptation, and an optional thigh-cuff for knee control. Go Plus extends this with multi-channel stimulation for combined foot-drop + knee-instability presentations (relevant where a participant also shows knee hyperextension in stance, not just swing-phase foot drop). If citing an upgrade, justify each functional gain separately — assessors will ask why the simpler/cheaper tier is insufficient for this participant specifically.
