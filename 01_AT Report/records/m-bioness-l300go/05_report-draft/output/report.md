## 1. Executive Summary

### Purpose of Report

This report has been prepared to support funding of the Bioness L300 Go Functional Electrical Stimulation (FES) System, including both the Lower Leg and Thigh Components, for Megan, a participant living with Multiple Sclerosis (MS). The recommendation is based on comprehensive orthotic assessment, in-clinic FES trial, eight-week community trial, independent physiotherapy outcome measures, participant and carer feedback, neurologist endorsement, three-week washout findings and current evidence relating to Functional Electrical Stimulation in Multiple Sclerosis. The purpose of this report is to determine whether permanent provision of the Bioness L300 Go represents a clinically appropriate, effective and reasonable and necessary support under the National Disability Insurance Scheme (NDIS).

### Participant Overview

Megan is a 36-year-old woman living with Multiple Sclerosis who experiences significant functional limitations associated with right lower limb weakness, foot drop, gait instability, fatigue and reduced walking efficiency. Clinical assessment identified:

- Right ankle dorsiflexion weakness
- Foot drop during swing phase
- Ankle inversion during gait
- Knee hyperextension during stance
- Reduced standing tolerance
- Fatigue-related activity limitation
- Reduced community participation

Importantly, Megan is not unable to walk. Rather, she currently maintains mobility through substantial activity restriction, continuous compensatory gait strategies, careful pacing of activities and avoidance of situations that increase fatigue, instability or falls risk. The absence of frequent falls should not be interpreted as absence of risk. Megan actively modifies her activities, walking environments and daily routines in order to maintain safety.

### Summary of Assessment Process

**Stage 1 – Comprehensive Assessment.** A detailed orthotic assessment identified disability-related impairments including foot drop, knee hyperextension, gait inefficiency, fatigue and participation restrictions associated with Multiple Sclerosis.

**Stage 2 – In-Clinic Trial.** The Bioness Lower Leg Component was trialled in clinic and demonstrated: improved swing phase foot clearance, improved gait quality, improved walking efficiency, reduced reliance on compensatory gait strategies, improved confidence during walking.

**Stage 3 – Community Trial.** An eight-week community trial was completed using both the Lower Leg and Thigh Components. Observed benefits included: improved walking efficiency, improved foot clearance, reduced knee hyperextension, increased endurance, improved standing tolerance, increased community participation, improved confidence, reduced fatigue-related activity restriction.

**Stage 4 – Washout Period.** Following completion of the community trial, the device was removed and a three-week washout period was completed. Following device removal: walking required substantially greater effort, reliance on compensatory gait strategies increased, confidence reduced, fatigue increased, participation decreased, near-fall events increased.

These findings demonstrate that the improvements achieved during the trial were directly attributable to the Bioness system and were not solely the result of symptom fluctuation, natural recovery or familiarity with outcome measures.

### Clinical Significance

The most important outcome was not improvement in walking speed alone. The community trial demonstrated that the Bioness L300 Go reduced the physical and cognitive burden associated with walking and enabled Megan to participate more fully in everyday activities. Megan currently maintains mobility through significant activity restriction, continuous compensatory strategies and avoidance of meaningful activities that increase fatigue or instability. The community trial demonstrated that the Bioness L300 Go substantially reduced these restrictions and enabled participation in activities that could not be achieved through existing supports alone.

The observed benefits were demonstrated through: objective outcome measures, community participation data, daily logbook records, participant feedback, carer feedback, neurologist endorsement, washout comparison findings.

### Recommendation Summary

Based on the totality of evidence presented within this report, it is my professional opinion that funding for the following assistive technology is both clinically justified and necessary:

**Recommended Equipment** — Bioness L300 Go Functional Electrical Stimulation System, including: Lower Leg Component, Thigh Component, Electrodes, Initial Setup and Programming, Clinical Fitting, Follow-Up Reviews, Ongoing Clinical Optimisation.

The device has demonstrated meaningful participant-specific benefits in mobility, endurance, confidence, safety and participation and is recommended as High Cost Assistive Technology under the NDIS.

## 6. Alternative Solutions Considered

### 6.1 Overview

A range of intervention options were considered prior to recommending the Bioness L300 Go Functional Electrical Stimulation System. Each option was evaluated against Megan's identified functional needs, trial outcomes and long-term mobility goals.

### 6.2 Option 1 — Continue Existing Supports Only

Existing supports include: physiotherapy, four-wheeled walker, neurological management, informal family support. These supports remain important and should continue. However, assessment findings demonstrate that existing supports alone have not adequately addressed: foot drop, reduced foot clearance, knee hyperextension, fatigue-related walking limitations. Consequently, this option was considered insufficient.

### 6.3 Option 2 — Continue Four-Wheeled Walker Alone

The four-wheeled walker provides stability and remains an important component of Megan's mobility strategy. However, the walker: does not restore ankle dorsiflexion, does not improve active foot clearance, does not address neurological gait impairments, does not reduce knee hyperextension. The walker therefore assists compensation but does not directly address the underlying impairments responsible for gait dysfunction.

### 6.4 Option 3 — Off-the-Shelf Ankle Foot Orthoses (AFOs)

Megan previously trialled an OAPL Supra-Lite AFO. The intervention was unsuccessful due to: poor fit, limb shape considerations, comfort concerns, bulkiness, difficulty accommodating spasticity. Off-the-shelf AFO management was therefore considered but demonstrated limited practicality for this participant. Additionally, a passive orthosis would not provide the active neuromuscular assistance observed during FES use.

### 6.5 Option 4 — Custom AFO

A custom AFO was considered. Potential benefits include: mechanical foot positioning, improved foot clearance, stability. However, custom AFO management would not: actively stimulate muscle contraction, provide neuromuscular re-education, address gait performance to the same extent demonstrated during trial, replicate participant-specific outcomes achieved during community use of Bioness.

A custom AFO was considered as a lower-cost alternative. However, a passive AFO would provide mechanical positioning rather than active, gait-timed neuromuscular stimulation and would not directly address stance-phase knee hyperextension. Based on Megan's previous orthotic experience, identified spasticity, participant-specific functional needs and the outcomes demonstrated during the FES trial, a custom AFO was not expected to reproduce the combined ankle, knee and participation outcomes achieved with the Bioness system.

### 6.6 Option 5 — Functional Electrical Stimulation

Functional Electrical Stimulation was investigated through: in-clinic trial, eight-week community trial, three-week washout comparison. This option demonstrated measurable participant-specific benefit across multiple domains. Consequently, FES emerged as the most effective intervention evaluated during the assessment process.

### 6.7 Conclusion of Alternatives Assessment

The Bioness L300 Go was the only intervention that demonstrated meaningful improvements in: walking efficiency, endurance, participation, confidence, community mobility, functional performance — while simultaneously addressing the neurological impairments responsible for Megan's mobility limitations.

## 7. Clinical Reasoning and Device Selection

### 7.1 Clinical Need for Functional Electrical Stimulation

Functional Electrical Stimulation is an established clinical intervention for management of drop foot arising from central neurological conditions and has demonstrated walking-related benefits in people with Multiple Sclerosis (Barrett et al., 2009; Street et al., 2015; NICE, 2009).

Assessment findings identified two major contributors to Megan's mobility limitations:

1. **Foot Drop and Reduced Foot Clearance** — associated with dorsiflexion weakness, reduced swing phase control, increased compensatory effort, fatigue-related mobility restriction.
2. **Knee Hyperextension** — associated with reduced gait efficiency, abnormal stance mechanics, increased energy expenditure, reduced endurance.

The proposed intervention was therefore required to address both impairments.

### 7.2 Rationale for Lower Leg Component

The Lower Leg Component was selected to address: foot drop, reduced swing phase foot clearance, ankle inversion, walking inefficiency. Clinical trial findings demonstrated immediate improvements in: foot clearance, walking quality, confidence, endurance.

### 7.3 Rationale for Thigh Component

The Thigh Component was selected to address: knee hyperextension during stance, reduced lower limb control, fatigue-related gait deterioration. Community trial findings demonstrated improved knee control and improved walking efficiency when the Thigh Component was incorporated. Provision of the Lower Leg Component alone would not adequately address all identified gait impairments.

The Lower Leg Component primarily addresses ankle dorsiflexion, swing phase foot clearance and ankle positioning. It does not directly address the stance-phase knee hyperextension identified during assessment. The Thigh Component was therefore not included as an optional enhancement, but as a clinically necessary component to address a separate proximal gait impairment.

During the community trial, Megan reported improved control of knee flexion and extension during standing and functional activities following optimisation of the thigh stimulation settings. Knee hyperextension was not observed during active use of the combined system, while intermittent knee hyperextension and associated knee pain re-emerged following device removal during the washout period. These participant-specific findings support the need for both components rather than provision of the Lower Leg Component alone.

### 7.4 Why Bioness L300 Go Was Selected

The Bioness L300 Go offers several clinical advantages relevant to Megan's presentation: dynamic real-time gait response, active dorsiflexion assistance, management of inversion tendency, adaptability to fluctuating neurological symptoms, ability to address both foot drop and knee instability when combined with the Thigh Component. These characteristics align closely with the impairments identified during assessment.

### 7.5 Participant-Specific Clinical Reasoning

Megan currently maintains mobility through substantial compensatory effort, careful pacing and avoidance of situations that increase fatigue or instability. This should not be interpreted as absence of need. Rather, it reflects the extent to which Megan limits participation in order to remain safe. The community trial demonstrated that the Bioness L300 Go substantially reduced these restrictions and enabled participation in activities that could not be achieved through existing supports alone. This participant-specific evidence provides the strongest justification for ongoing provision of the device.

## 9. Community Trial Methodology

### 9.1 Trial Overview

Following the successful in-clinic trial, Megan completed an eight-week community trial of the Bioness L300 Go Functional Electrical Stimulation System. The trial was conducted between 18 March 2026 and 11 May 2026. A subsequent three-week washout period was completed between 12 May 2026 and 1 June 2026. The purpose of the trial was to evaluate the effectiveness of the Bioness system within real-world environments and determine whether long-term provision was clinically justified.

### 9.2 Equipment Trialled

The community trial incorporated:
- **Bioness L300 Go Lower Leg Component**, to address foot drop, reduced foot clearance, ankle inversion during gait.
- **Bioness L300 Go Thigh Component**, to address knee hyperextension, reduced lower limb control during stance.

Both components were used throughout the community trial.

### 9.3 Trial Environments

The device was used across a broad range of environments representative of Megan's normal daily activities, including: home environments, community footpaths, shopping centres, medical appointments, hospital visits, family outings, indoor and outdoor walking environments, ramps, steps, uneven surfaces. This ensured that the trial reflected real-world performance rather than isolated clinic-based observations.

### 9.4 Monitoring and Review

Throughout the trial period, progress was monitored through: orthotist review, physiotherapy outcome measures, participant logbooks, washout comparison, participant feedback, carer feedback, neurologist review.

### 9.5 Outcome Measures

All standardised outcome measures reported within this assessment were administered and recorded by the treating physiotherapist at baseline, during the community trial and following the washout period. These measures were selected to evaluate mobility, endurance, dynamic balance, lower limb function, fatigue and participant-perceived functional performance.

Measures completed included:
- **Timed Up and Go Test (TUG)** — assessment of functional mobility and mobility efficiency.
- **Six Minute Walk Test (6MWT)** — assessment of walking endurance and exercise tolerance.
- **Dynamic Gait Index (DGI)** — assessment of dynamic balance and gait performance across functional walking tasks.
- **Thirty Second Sit-to-Stand Test (30STS)** — assessment of lower limb functional performance.
- **Fatigue Severity Scale (FSS)** — assessment of fatigue-related impact on daily activities and participation.
- **Patient Specific Functional Scale (PSFS)** — assessment of participant-perceived performance in meaningful daily activities affected by disability.

Additional information was obtained through: community trial logbooks, washout logbooks, participant feedback, carer feedback, neurologist review, orthotic assessment observations.

A three-week washout period was incorporated to determine whether observed improvements were maintained following removal of the device and to further evaluate participant-specific benefit.

### 9.6 Importance of Washout Phase

A three-week washout period was intentionally incorporated into the assessment process. The purpose of the washout period was to determine whether improvements observed during the trial remained present after device removal. This provided a stronger level of participant-specific evidence than would be available through outcome measures alone.

## 15. Research Evidence Supporting the Recommendation

### 15.2 Evidence Supporting Functional Electrical Stimulation

Published research has examined the use of Functional Electrical Stimulation for management of foot drop and walking impairment in people with Multiple Sclerosis. A randomised trial by Barrett et al. (2009) investigated FES and therapeutic exercise in people with Multiple Sclerosis and demonstrated an orthotic benefit associated with FES use during walking. This study supports the use of FES as a functional intervention for disability-related foot drop and impaired walking performance. Street et al. (2015) further reported that FES was associated with improvement in walking speed and clinically meaningful changes in walking performance in people with Multiple Sclerosis. More broadly, published evidence and clinical guidance support potential benefits including: improved walking speed, improved walking performance, improved swing-phase foot clearance, improved gait efficiency, reduced effort associated with walking, improved functional mobility, increased confidence during mobility.

NICE guidance supports the use of FES for drop foot of central neurological origin where the participant is appropriately assessed and the intervention is provided with suitable clinical governance, fitting and follow-up arrangements (NICE, 2009).

### 15.3 Consistency Between Research and Trial Findings

The outcomes demonstrated during Megan's trial are consistent with the published evidence supporting FES for management of foot drop and walking impairment in people with Multiple Sclerosis. Barrett et al. (2009) identified an orthotic effect associated with FES use during walking, while Street et al. (2015) reported improvements in walking speed and clinically meaningful walking performance. These findings are consistent with Megan's participant-specific improvements in: Ten Metre Walk Test performance, Timed Up and Go performance, walking endurance, swing-phase foot clearance, walking efficiency, confidence during mobility.

Although published evidence supports the use of FES for neurological foot drop and walking impairment in people with Multiple Sclerosis (Barrett et al., 2009; Street et al., 2015; NICE, 2009), NDIS funding decisions must primarily consider whether the proposed support is effective and beneficial for the individual participant.
