What kind of information do i need to gathering to write NDIS Assistive technology report after 2 month trial of bioness for MS patient?

Great question — a strong NDIS Assistive Technology (AT) report after a 2-month Bioness trial for a client with Multiple Sclerosis needs to clearly show need, evidence of benefit, and value for money.

Here’s exactly what information you should gather and include:

1. Participant & Context
NDIS number
Diagnosis: Multiple Sclerosis (type if known)
Functional impacts (mobility, fatigue, balance, falls risk)
Living situation (home layout, community access needs)
Current mobility aids (AFO, cane, walker, wheelchair, etc.)
2. Baseline (Before Trial)

You must show what life was like before Bioness.

Mobility & Function
Walking distance (e.g. 20m, 100m, community only)
Gait pattern: foot drop severity, circumduction, hip hiking
Speed (10MWT if available)
Endurance (6MWT if available)
Falls history (frequency, near-falls)
Fatigue level (subjective scale)
Participation
Ability to:
Shop independently
Walk outdoors
Attend work / appointments
Manage stairs or uneven surfaces
3. Trial Details

Document the structure of the trial.

Dates of trial (start–end)
Bioness model used
Wearing schedule (hours/day, days/week)
Training provided (who, when)
Support required (carer set-up vs independent)
Any issues: skin, tolerance, spasticity, tech problems
4. Outcomes After 2 Months

This is the most important section.

Objective Changes (if available)
Walking speed improvement
Increased distance
Reduced tripping
Improved foot clearance
Better symmetry
Reduced reliance on walking aids
Subjective Changes

From participant, carer, and clinician:

Confidence walking
Reduced fear of falling
Fatigue change
Pain change
Willingness to go out more
Quality of life impact
5. Comparison to Other Options

NDIS needs to see why Bioness is reasonable and necessary.

Compare against:

AFO
No device
Physiotherapy alone

Include:

Why AFO was insufficient
Why Bioness is superior for THIS client
Long-term benefits vs short-term therapy only
6. Risk & Safety
Falls risk with and without device
Safety on stairs, ramps, community terrain
Risk of deconditioning if not approved
Carer safety (less physical support needed)
7. Independence & Participation Outcomes

Link directly to NDIS goals:

Increased community access
Return to work/study
Reduced carer support
Increased self-management
Maintaining function as MS progresses
8. Cost & Value for Money

Include:

Cost of Bioness system
Expected lifespan
Cost comparison:
Ongoing physio
Falls injuries
Increased support hours
Show long-term savings
9. Clinical Recommendation

Clear statement:

Device is reasonable and necessary
Not luxury, not convenience — essential mobility support
Consequences if not funded
10. Supporting Evidence to Attach
Trial outcome summary
Gait assessment
Physio letter
Falls record
Photos/video (if allowed)
Participant testimonial

NDIS Assistive Technology Report

Bioness L300 Go Trial – Multiple Sclerosis

1. Participant Details

Name: Jane Smith
NDIS Number: 4312 567 890
Date of Birth: 14 March 1989
Primary Diagnosis: Relapsing-Remitting Multiple Sclerosis
Referring Clinician: Dr Michael Brown, Neurologist
Report Author: Mee Young Lee, Orthotist
Date of Report: 13 January 2026

2. Purpose of Report

This report is submitted to support an application for NDIS funding for a Bioness L300 Go Functional Electrical Stimulation (FES) system following a two-month trial period. The trial aimed to evaluate the impact of the device on the participant’s gait efficiency, safety, independence, and community participation.

3. Participant Background

Jane lives independently in a single-storey home and works part-time as an administrative assistant. She experiences progressive right-sided weakness associated with MS, resulting in significant foot drop, reduced walking endurance, and frequent near-falls.

She currently receives:

Physiotherapy: once weekly
OT support: monthly
No formal carer support

Current mobility aids include a carbon fibre AFO and a single-point cane for community ambulation.

Primary functional concerns include:

High fatigue levels after short walking distances
Reduced confidence in outdoor mobility
Fear of falling, particularly on uneven surfaces
Reduced community engagement
4. Baseline Function (Pre-Trial)
Mobility
Walking distance: ~60–80 m before requiring rest
Gait pattern: Right foot drop with circumduction and hip hiking
Walking aids: AFO + cane outdoors
Falls history: 3 falls and multiple near-falls in the previous 6 months
10-Metre Walk Test: 0.68 m/s
Functional Impact

Jane reported limiting community outings due to fear of tripping and fatigue. She avoided uneven ground and required frequent rest breaks when shopping or attending appointments. Confidence with mobility was low, particularly when fatigued.

5. Trial Overview
Device trialled: Bioness L300 Go
Trial period: 10 November 2025 – 10 January 2026
Set-up & training: Provided by orthotist with physiotherapy input
Wearing schedule:
Week 1–2: 1–2 hours/day
Week 3 onwards: up to 6 hours/day in community and work settings
Independence: Jane was able to don/doff the device independently after 1 week
Issues during trial:
Mild skin redness initially, resolved with strap adjustment
No adverse effects or intolerance
6. Outcomes After 2 Months
Objective Outcomes
Walking speed: Improved from 0.68 m/s to 0.86 m/s
Walking distance: Increased from ~80 m to over 200 m without rest
Gait quality:
Improved foot clearance
Reduced circumduction
Improved step symmetry
Falls: No falls during the 2-month trial period
Walking aids: Reduced reliance on cane in familiar indoor environments
Subjective Outcomes

Participant report:

“I feel much safer walking now. I don’t have to constantly think about lifting my foot. I’m less tired at the end of the day and I’ve started going out more again.”

Jane reported:

Increased confidence in outdoor walking
Reduced fear of falling
Improved endurance
Greater willingness to attend social and community activities

Physiotherapist feedback:

Improved gait efficiency and reduced compensatory movements
Improved dynamic balance during walking
Increased tolerance for therapy sessions due to reduced fatigue
7. Comparison to Other Interventions
AFO

While Jane’s AFO provides passive ankle support, it:

Does not address fatigue-related foot drop progression
Restricts ankle movement and limits dynamic gait
Has contributed to increased compensatory movements at the hip
Physiotherapy Alone

Physiotherapy has improved strength and balance but has not been sufficient to manage the neurological component of foot drop, particularly as fatigue increases throughout the day.

Why Bioness Is Most Appropriate

The Bioness system provides active, timed dorsiflexion, supporting a more natural gait pattern and reducing compensatory strategies. It adapts well to day-to-day variability in MS symptoms and enables functional carryover into daily life.

8. Risk & Safety Considerations

Without Bioness:

High risk of falls due to inconsistent foot clearance
Increased fatigue leading to gait deterioration
Reduced community participation and physical deconditioning

With Bioness:

Improved stability on stairs, ramps, and uneven surfaces
Reduced falls risk
Improved confidence and safety in community ambulation
Reduced reliance on others for supervision
9. Impact on Independence & NDIS Goals

The Bioness system directly supports Jane’s NDIS goals to:

Maintain independence in daily mobility
Increase community participation
Sustain employment
Reduce reliance on informal supports
Maintain physical function despite progressive neurological changes

Since commencing the trial, Jane has increased her weekly community outings and resumed independent grocery shopping without assistance.

10. Cost & Value for Money
Cost of Bioness L300 Go: $9,500 (estimate)
Expected lifespan: 4–5 years
Ongoing costs: Replacement electrodes and servicing
Value for Money

Funding this device is expected to:

Reduce the need for increased physiotherapy input
Reduce falls-related injury risk and associated medical costs
Delay progression to higher-level mobility aids
Reduce future need for support worker assistance

Over the lifespan of the device, this represents significant cost avoidance compared to ongoing support hours and injury management.

11. Clinical Recommendation

Based on the outcomes of the two-month trial, it is strongly recommended that the NDIS approve funding for the Bioness L300 Go system for Jane Smith.

The device has demonstrated clear benefits in:

Improving gait safety and efficiency
Reducing falls risk
Increasing independence and community participation
Supporting long-term functional mobility in the context of progressive MS

Without this device, Jane is at increased risk of:

Reduced mobility and confidence
Increased falls and injury
Greater reliance on formal and informal supports
Reduced quality of life and participation

The Bioness system therefore meets the NDIS criteria of being reasonable and necessary.

12. Attachments
Physiotherapy outcome summary
Gait assessment results
Participant statement
Falls record (pre-trial)
