Bioness L300 Go Functional Electrical Stimulation System

Participant: M
Diagnosis: Multiple Sclerosis
Primary impairment: Right foot drop secondary to MS
Recommended AT: Bioness L300 Go FES System
AT category: High Cost Assistive Technology, over $15,000

Executive Summary

This report supports NDIS funding for the long-term provision of the Bioness L300 Go Functional Electrical Stimulation system for M, a 35-year-old participant with Multiple Sclerosis who presents with significant right-sided foot drop, ankle inversion, gait instability, fatigue and reduced community participation.

M completed an in-clinic trial, an 8-week community trial and a 3-week washout period. The trial demonstrated clinically meaningful improvements in walking endurance, gait safety, confidence, community access and independence. The removal of the device resulted in deterioration in walking confidence, fatigue, tripping risk and participation, confirming that the benefit was directly related to access to the Bioness L300 Go.

Objective outcomes included:

Measure	Pre-trial	End of trial	Outcome
TUG	42.57 sec	33.11 sec	22% faster
6MWT	57 m	90 m	58% increase
DGI	11/24	14/24	improved dynamic gait
Falls during active trial	—	0 reported	improved safety
Washout	—	increased trips/near falls	deterioration without device

These outcomes support that the Bioness L300 Go is effective, beneficial, related to disability, value for money and the most appropriate AT option for M.

Participant Background and Disability-Related Need

M has a confirmed diagnosis of Multiple Sclerosis. Her MS-related neurological impairment causes right lower limb weakness, right foot drop, ankle inversion, reduced walking endurance, fatigue and increased risk of tripping.

Clinical findings include:

Right ankle dorsiflexion: 0/5
Right ankle eversion: 0/5
Right ankle inversion: 1/5
Right plantarflexion: 3/5
Knee flexion/extension: 4/5
Moderate gastrocnemius and soleus spasticity
Right foot drop and inversion during swing phase
Right knee hyperextension during stance
Toe curling with prolonged standing/walking
Reliance on 4-wheeled walker indoors
Restricted community access due to fatigue and fall risk

These findings are consistent with the uploaded clinical report and previous community trial application.

Previous AT and Why It Is Insufficient

M trialled an off-the-shelf OAPL Supra-Lite AFO. This was unsuccessful due to poor fit, bulkiness, discomfort, spasticity and mild equinus contracture. It did not adequately address ankle inversion, fatigue-related deterioration or functional walking confidence.

A custom AFO may provide passive positioning, but it does not actively stimulate dorsiflexion or eversion, does not support neuromuscular activation and does not address MS-related motor fatigue in the same way as FES.

Physiotherapy alone is not sufficient because M has 0/5 voluntary ankle dorsiflexion and requires external neuromuscular facilitation during gait.

Bioness L300 Go Trial Outcome
In-clinic trial

During the in-clinic trial, M demonstrated immediate improvement in:

foot clearance during swing phase
stance phase stability
walking speed
walking distance
perceived fatigue
walking confidence

The original supporting letter documented improved foot clearance, improved stability, increased walking speed/distance and reduced fatigue during the short trial.

8-week community trial

During the 8-week community trial, M demonstrated improvement in real-world environments including home, appointments, shopping, family outings and community walking.

The most significant objective change was the 6MWT improvement from 57m to 90m, representing a 58% increase in walking endurance. TUG improved from 42.57 seconds to 33.11 seconds. DGI improved from 11/24 to 14/24.

Washout period

Following removal of the device, M experienced increased fatigue, stiffness, tripping, reduced confidence, reduced participation and psychological deterioration. This is important because it confirms that the improvement was not incidental; it was directly associated with access to the Bioness L300 Go.

Research Evidence Supporting FES for MS-Related Foot Drop

A systematic review and meta-analysis found that FES for foot drop in people with MS has positive initial and ongoing effects on gait speed in short walking tests.

A broader MS FES literature review reported benefits beyond walking speed, including improved quality and safety of walking, reduced effort, reduced fatigue and improved user satisfaction.

NDIS guidance states that evidence for high-cost AT may include product trials, previous AT use, research or literature reviews and expert opinion. For high-cost AT over $15,000, NDIS requires assessment evidence from an assessor.

These research findings align strongly with M’s trial results: improved walking endurance, improved safety, reduced fatigue and functional deterioration when the device was removed.

NDIS Reasonable and Necessary Justification
1. Related to disability

The requested AT directly relates to M’s MS. Her foot drop, ankle inversion, fatigue and gait instability are neurological consequences of MS. The Bioness L300 Go directly addresses these impairments by stimulating the common peroneal nerve to assist dorsiflexion and foot clearance during gait.

2. Effective and beneficial

The device has demonstrated clear functional benefit during both in-clinic and community trials. M’s 6MWT improved by 58%, TUG improved by 22%, DGI improved from 11/24 to 14/24 and no falls were reported during the active trial period. Functional deterioration during washout further confirms benefit.

3. Value for money

Although the device is high cost, the trial demonstrates that it is likely to reduce longer-term support needs by improving mobility, reducing fall risk, reducing carer reliance and supporting ongoing participation. Funding the device is more cost-effective than unmanaged deterioration, increased falls risk, increased support worker hours or avoidable injury.

4. Most appropriate support

Alternative options have been considered and are insufficient. The off-the-shelf AFO failed. A custom AFO is passive and does not provide active neuromuscular stimulation. Physiotherapy alone cannot compensate for absent dorsiflexion strength. No intervention would leave M at high risk of falls, reduced mobility and increased dependence.

5. Supports NDIS goals

The Bioness L300 Go supports M’s goals to increase mobility, improve stability, reduce falls risk, increase independence, improve home safety and participate more confidently in community life.

Clinical Recommendation

Based on the clinical assessment, failed previous orthotic trial, positive in-clinic trial, 8-week community trial outcomes, washout deterioration and supporting research evidence, it is my professional opinion that the Bioness L300 Go Functional Electrical Stimulation system is clinically justified and should be funded by the NDIS as ongoing high-cost assistive technology.

The device is reasonable and necessary because it directly addresses M’s disability-related mobility impairment, has demonstrated measurable and meaningful functional benefit, supports her NDIS goals, reduces risk and is the most appropriate intervention currently available.

Recommendation:
NDIS funding is strongly recommended for the purchase, fitting, training, implementation and follow-up review of the Bioness L300 Go FES system.
